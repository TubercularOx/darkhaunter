Predictions worth recording

1. Ruck predicts the MitD reveal will happen during the final battle.

Basis:
Ruck says he is presuming the reveal will be during the final battle, and later says he still thinks it is going to happen during the final battle.

Use:
If the reveal happens before the final battle, even by a meaningful scene or sequence, this is directly usable.

2. Ruck predicts MitD will not meet another member of its species before the reveal.

Basis:
Ruck says he feels pretty confident it will not happen before MitD’s reveal, because it risks spoiling the reveal. He also says he does not think MitD will meet one of his species in the final dungeon and say something like “Oh, you look just like me!”

Use:
This is better than “he thinks it would be bad storytelling,” because it is testable.

3. Ruck thinks a MitD species encounter is only plausibly acceptable in the denouement, and even then he does not expect it.

Basis:
Ruck allows that after Xykon is vanquished, Redcloak makes a deal, and the world is saved, MitD could encounter others of its kind in the denouement, but says he does not think that will happen.

Use:
Useful if Rich uses another member of MitD’s species earlier than denouement.

4. Ruck predicts MitD will switch sides during the climactic battle, as a pivotal moment.

Basis:
Ruck says he thinks MitD will ultimately change sides and join the good guys, and that this will be a pivotal moment in the climactic battle.

Use:
Useful if MitD’s side-switch is effectively done before the climax, or if the decisive moment is not where he expects.

5. Ruck now predicts MitD will participate at least until the final battle is finished, probably not exit immediately afterward, and probably not die in that battle.

Basis:
When pressed after denying the “leave the story” prediction, he says he will predict “at least until the battle is finished.” He adds that he does not think MitD just exits stage left immediately after it ends, and thinks MitD is too strong for death in the last battle to be realistic.

Use:
Less valuable for contradiction, but valuable as a replacement record after his denial.

6. Ruck predicts that, at this late stage, the party is more likely to stay together than split apart again.

Basis:
After the structural post, he answers that at this late stage the party is more likely to stay together than split apart again.

Use:
A very useful fresh prediction. It is direct, current, and tied to his answer to “what feels most like Rich?”

7. Ruck doubts the Team Evil conflict will split into more than one battle, mainly because he does not see how the groups disengage and re-engage.

Basis:
Ruck says he is struggling to see how the conflict could be split into multiple battles. Later he says his skepticism is based on practicality: one side would have to disengage and retreat, and he is not sure who would, especially if Team Evil has a falling out.

Use:
More a position than a prediction, but likely to become useful if Rich creates a separation by any unexpected mechanism.

8. Ruck believes Hinjo has to show up again, and treats #665b as requiring it.

Basis:
Ruck says Hinjo is one situation where the reveal could happen before the finale, because Rich has commented that Hinjo has a major role left and Ruck believes he is on his way north. Later, when asked for a citation, he says one of the book commentaries, and adds that #665b simply cannot happen if Hinjo does not show up again.

Use:
Worth recording, but not central to the current argument unless Hinjo becomes involved in the rift / Redcloak / finale structure.


Positions worth recording

1. Ruck acknowledges that Rich may make an apparently unlikely element work for reasons Ruck has not thought of.

Basis:
Ruck says that if MitD meeting another member of its species happens, he has faith Rich will have a reason for it that he has not thought of. Later he generalizes that, in theory, Rich could do anything for any reason, and says he is open to the possibility that there would be a reason for a storytelling element he has not thought of.

Use:
This is the most valuable “future contradiction” position. Use it when he says something cannot work because he cannot see how.

2. Ruck’s discussion standard is that a hypothesis needs an affirmative defense before it is worth considering.

Basis:
Ruck says if someone suggests something and cannot come up with a good reason why, “why should anyone bother considering it?” He also says the claim requires “affirmative defense, in all cases,” and that if someone cannot make the case, he does not see why anyone else should consider it or make the case for them.

Use:
Likely to conflict with the “Rich can have a reason I haven’t thought of” position.

3. Ruck rejects “wouldn’t it be cool if...” as a valid story reason.

Basis:
Ruck says he is well and repeatedly on the record that he does not think “wouldn’t it be cool if...” is a good reason. Earlier he rejects “wouldn’t it be cool if everything was connected?” and “wouldn’t it be dramatic for the MitD to meet his dad?” as sufficient reasons.

Use:
Useful if he later praises exactly that sort of connection once Rich makes it work.

4. Ruck’s principle: late elements that affect climactic choices are generally not good storytelling if they detract from the main story or climax.

Basis:
Ruck says, as a principle, that introducing elements late that detract from the main story and impact of the climax is generally not good storytelling. He specifically says it would not be good if MitD made an important climactic choice because of a random element not yet introduced.

Use:
Valuable because “random element,” “late,” and “detract” are all movable labels. Track how he applies them.

5. Ruck’s position: MitD’s climactic choice should come from his established relationship with O-Chul and the Order, not from a late encounter with another member of his species.

Basis:
Ruck says another member of MitD’s species could muddy the motivations and significance of MitD’s choice. Later he says such a meeting could cheapen the journey MitD has been on, because the motivation should come from O-Chul and MitD’s established relationships rather than a random/coincidental species encounter.

Use:
Likely to become useful if Rich uses species identity, ancestry, or another monster as part of the reveal without replacing O-Chul’s role.

6. Ruck’s position: stories need connective tissue, but connecting details for their own sake is weak or contrived.

Basis:
Ruck says stories need connective tissue, otherwise they are arbitrary, but also says Rich has said conservation of detail is overrated. He frames the problem as connecting disparate things “for its own sake,” or introducing an element where it does not make sense just because it was mentioned earlier.

Use:
This is the Scylla and Charybdis axis. Track when he calls one connection too connected and another not connected enough.

7. Ruck claims his judgments are grounded in long study of storytelling and in dramatic principles he thinks apply to OOTS.

Basis:
In response to “If we don’t have a good grasp on how Rich writes,” Ruck says, “I think I kinda do,” because he has been a student of storytelling for a long time and OOTS uses dramatic principles that are fundamental across millennia.

Use:
This raises the stakes of his predictions. He is not merely saying “my guess”; he is investing interpretive authority.

8. When challenged on what feels like Rich, Ruck shifts his objection toward Watsonian practicality.

Basis:
After the “what feels most like Rich?” post, Ruck says his skepticism is “more Watsonian than Doylist,” focused on who disengages and why.

Use:
If he later returns to “bad storytelling” or “Rich wouldn’t,” this is precedent that he retreated from Doylist authority into practical logistics when pressed.

9. Ruck denies having predicted that MitD would leave the story as soon as it reveals itself.

Basis:
Ruck directly says “I made no such prediction” when asked if he would stick with his prediction that MitD leaves the story as soon as it reveals itself.

Use:
Since there is memory of a prior version, track this separately. Do not rely on memory next time; store the quote when found.
